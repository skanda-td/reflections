Circle Theory

2026-04-05

Let’s imagine a person standing inside a circle. After observing the circle from within and seeing its boundaries, they feel they have understood everything about it. However, when the same person steps outside the circle, they realize it was only a smaller circle, and that there is a much bigger circle beyond it.

What this means is that we often assume we understand the full picture of a topic—whether it’s business, the human mind, or a concept. But when we are exposed to a broader perspective, we realize that our understanding was incomplete.

After seeing the above-stated idea, a way of approach to see topics or things would look like this: if we want to fully understand the smaller circle, we need to explore beyond it. By learning what lies outside, we gain a deeper and more accurate understanding of what is inside.

Real-World Parallels

  • Psychology
    • Dunning–Kruger Effect: People with low knowledge tend to overestimate their understanding, while those with high knowledge recognize their limitations.
    • Cognitive Bias (Illusion of Knowledge): Humans assume patterns = full understanding; we fill gaps unknowingly.
    • Metacognition: True intelligence = knowing what you don’t know.
  • Philosophy
    • Socratic Wisdom: “I know that I know nothing." Recognizing ignorance = higher knowledge.
    • Plato’s Cave: People think shadows are reality. Leaving cave = seeing larger truth.
    • Epistemology (study of knowledge): Knowledge is always partial, contextual, evolving.

Limitations

  • There is no “final biggest circle” → Knowledge is infinite.
  • Not all expansion leads to clarity → Can also cause confusion without structure.
  • Requires reflection → Just exposure ≠ understanding.

End Note: We mistake limited perspective for complete knowledge; only by stepping beyond it do we understand both its limits and its depth.
